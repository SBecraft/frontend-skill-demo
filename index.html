Maryland Quick Facts:

  • Maryland named the blue crab its state crustacean in 1989, and most people just call it the "Maryland crab."
  • The Maryland State Police began in 1921 and is considered the oldest statewide law enforcement agency in the United States.
  • Maryland's football team, the Baltimore Ravens, are named after the poem "The Raven" written by Poet Edgar Allen Poe.
  • On October 1, 2008, Maryland became the first U.S. state to designate an official exercise: walking.

Maryland at a Glance


Maryland might be small, but it has a lot of variety packed into it. People often call it “America in Miniature” (What Is Maryland's Nickname? Unveiling the Old Line State's History–States Tale, 2023), and the nickname makes sense. You can go from beaches on the Atlantic coast to farmland and then to mountains in the western part of the state, all in a short drive. The Chesapeake Bay is a huge part of Maryland's identity and affects everything from the scenery to the food, especially the well–known steamed Maryland blue crabs.

Maryland also does well economically. It has the highest median household income in the country, and its poverty rate is one of the lowest. Education is another strong point, since most residents finish high school and the state ranks high for bachelor's degrees. Still, Maryland isn't perfect. Its unemployment rate is a bit higher than the national average at 4.2%, which shows that even strong economies have challenges (World Population Review, 2024). These details help explain why Maryland has a mix of strengths and issues at the same time.

Maryland's history and community life stand out too. As one of the original thirteen colonies, the state played an important role in early American history. You can still see that in places like Annapolis, which has colonial–era buildings and a strong maritime culture. Baltimore brings a different vibe, with its arts scene, innovation, and neighborhoods that each have their own personality. Across the state, from small towns with long–running traditions to busy cities, people take pride in their schools, local industries, and of course, their blue crabs. Whether you're exploring nature, learning about Maryland's past, or enjoying a crab feast, the state really does offer a little bit of everything.
